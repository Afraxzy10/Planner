Writing Templates & Structures
Your Trainer Says

Memorise these structures. Not the content — the skeleton. When you sit in the exam, you won't think about structure. You'll think about ideas. That's how you score Band 7.

Task 2 — Opinion Essay (Agree/Disagree)
INTRODUCTION (2 sentences): "It is argued that [paraphrase topic]. This essay [agrees/disagrees] with this view for the following reasons." BODY PARAGRAPH 1 (Point–Explain–Example): "The primary reason for this is [point]. This is because [explanation]. For example, [specific example with data or real case]." BODY PARAGRAPH 2: "Furthermore, [second point]. [Explanation]. A clear illustration of this is [example]." CONCLUSION (2 sentences): "In conclusion, this essay has argued that [restate your position]. Therefore, [recommendation or future prediction]." WORD TARGET: 270–290 words | TIME: 40 minutes max
Task 2 — Discussion Essay (Both Views)
INTRODUCTION: "There is ongoing debate about [topic]. While some argue [view 1], others believe [view 2]. This essay will examine both perspectives before reaching a conclusion." BODY 1 — First View: "Those who believe [view 1] argue that [point + explanation + example]." BODY 2 — Second View + Your Opinion: "On the other hand, proponents of [view 2] contend that [point + explanation + example]. In my opinion, [your view] because [reason]." CONCLUSION: "To summarise, although [view 1 has merit], [view 2] is more convincing because [reason]."
Task 1 — Bar Chart / Line Graph Language
OVERVIEW (most important paragraph — write this 2nd): "Overall, it is clear that [biggest trend]. [Second key feature]." DESCRIBING TRENDS: Rise: increased significantly / rose sharply / grew steadily / surged Fall: decreased dramatically / declined gradually / dropped sharply / fell Stable: remained relatively stable / showed little change / plateaued at Peak: reached a peak of / peaked at / hit a high of Low: fell to a low of / bottomed out at / reached its lowest point COMPARING: "X was considerably higher than Y..." "While A increased, B showed the opposite trend..." "Both X and Y experienced a similar pattern..." OPENING SENTENCE (paraphrase the title): "The [chart/graph] illustrates [what] in [where] between [dates]."
Task 1 — Process Diagram Language
SEQUENCE WORDS: First / Initially / To begin with / Then / Next / Following this / Subsequently / After that / Finally / Ultimately PASSIVE VOICE (use heavily in process diagrams): "The raw materials are collected..." / "The mixture is then heated..." "Once dried, the product is..." / "This is followed by..." OVERVIEW: "Overall, the process involves [number] main stages, beginning with [first step] and ending with [final step]."
Useful Academic Phrases for Band 7 Writing
GIVING OPINIONS: In my view / From my perspective / I would argue that ADDING IDEAS: Furthermore / Moreover / In addition / Additionally CONTRASTING: However / Nevertheless / On the other hand / Despite this GIVING EXAMPLES: For instance / For example / To illustrate / A case in point is CONCLUDING: In conclusion / To summarise / Overall / In summary CAUSE/EFFECT: As a result / Consequently / This leads to / Therefore / Thus CONCEDING: Although / While / Even though / Despite the fact that
Speaking Trainer Guide
The Speaking Secret

The examiner is NOT testing your knowledge or opinions. They are testing your English. You can say something simple in complex English and score Band 7. Stop trying to have "smart" ideas — focus on fluent, accurate delivery.

Part 1 — Personal Questions Strategy
Rule 1
Never answer with 1 sentence. Always give 2–3 sentences.
Bad: "I like reading." | Good: "I'm quite fond of reading, particularly non-fiction books about science. I find that reading helps me unwind after a long day, and I usually read for about 30 minutes before bed."
Rule 2
Use this structure: Direct Answer + Reason + Detail/Example
Q: Do you prefer mornings or evenings? → "I'd say I'm more of a morning person. I feel much more productive in the early hours — my mind is clearer and I can focus better. So I tend to schedule my most important tasks before noon."
Rule 3
Replace filler words with intelligent connectors
Instead of "um... uh...": use "Well...", "That's a good question...", "Let me think about that...", "I'd say...", "To be honest..."
Part 2 — Cue Card (2-minute monologue)
Structure
The P-E-E-L method for Part 2
Point (what) → Explain (why/how) → Experience (personal story) → Link (why it matters to you). Cover all bullet points on the card in your first 90 seconds. Use the last 30 seconds to expand on one point.
Prep time
Use your 1 minute wisely — make a mind map not sentences
Write 3–5 keywords only. Don't write full sentences — you'll just read them out robotically. Keywords trigger natural speech.
Practice topics
Top 20 most common cue card topics to practise
A place you visited / A person who influenced you / A book you read / A skill you want to learn / A happy memory / A piece of technology / A celebration / A journey / A time you helped someone / A teacher / A goal / A gift / A piece of news / A sport / A film / A piece of music / A change in your city / A website / A traditional food / A time you were late

Part 3 — Abstract Discussion Strategy
Structure
O-R-E-O for every Part 3 answer
Opinion → Reason → Example → Opinion (restated). Example: Q: "Do you think technology has changed the way people communicate?" → "I strongly believe it has transformed communication fundamentally. [R] The main reason is that digital platforms have removed geographical barriers. [E] For instance, someone in India can instantly video call a colleague in the US at no cost. [O] So yes, technology has made communication faster and more accessible than ever before."
Buy time
Legal ways to pause and think in Part 3
"That's quite a thought-provoking question..." / "I haven't really considered this before, but I'd say..." / "Well, from different angles, one could argue..." / "It really depends on how you look at it..."

Pronunciation — The Quick Win
  • Speak at 70% of your natural speed — examiners need to understand you
  • Stress the RIGHT syllable: PREsent (noun) vs preSENT (verb)
  • Link words together: "turn it on" → "turnit on"
  • Use rising intonation for questions, falling for statements
  • Practice tongue twisters for 2 min/day to improve articulation
